Attractions

Freedom Trail


Freedom Trail- The Freedom Trail is a 2.5-mile-long path through downtown Boston that passes by sixteen sites that are significant to the history of the nation and tell the story of the American Revolution. The historic sites include museums, churches, meeting houses, burying grounds, parks, a ship, and historic markers. The trail is marked largely with brick throughout the city in between modern pedestrian paths and streets. The trail is one the National Millennium Trail that winds between Boston Common to the Bunker Hill Monument in Charlestown. The trail is largely self-guided, you may also opt for guided tours available at various sites. Self-guided audio tour devices are also available at the Freedom Trail visitor center and through the app. Some of the iconic sites on the trail include the Massachusetts State House, King's Chapel & King's Chapel Burying Ground, Boston Massacre Site, Old Meeting House, Faneuil Hall, Copp's Hill Burying Ground, USS Constitution and the Bunker Hill Monument.

Fenway Park


Fenway park is the oldest ballpark, one of the most well-known and famous baseball parks located in Boston, home for the Boston Red Sox, the city's American League baseball team since 1912. Guided public tours of the stadium are available and relatively popular, helping make the ballpark one of Boston’s more popular tourist attractions. If you’re visiting during the game season, and lucky enough to get a ticket to a game, you should certainly catch a Red Sox game. If you can’t get tickets to a game, you can still grab some official Red Sox merchandise or grab a beer at the Bleader Bar, built under the stadium stands.

Cambridge


The adjacent city Situated directly north of Boston, across the Charles River is home to some of the most prestigious and well-known higher learning institutions of the world, Harvard University, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). Guided tours are available at the open and easily accessible campuses of both institutions. In addition to touring the campuses (e.g., the Harvard Yard), a handful of museums are also available on site. The museums include the Harvard Art Museum, Harvard Museum of Natural Science, Harvard Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology, Harvard Semitic Museum MIT museum and MIT List Visual Arts Center. Harvard Square is a triangular plaza adjacent to the Harvard Yard, with many restaurants, cafes and shops.

Boston Public Library and Copley Square


The Boston Public Library opened in 1852 as the nation’s first free municipal library is one of the most gorgeous libraries in the nation. The library is on the National Register of Historic Place. Specifically, its McKim building is well-known and famed for its coffered ceiling, arched pathways and lavish decorations draws over 2.2 million visitor every year from across the nation and around the world. Daily guided public tours are available. The library is situated on one side of the impressively imposing Copley Square (which also houses the Trinity Church). Copley square is famously known for the being the location of finish line for the annual Boston Marathon foot race, world's oldest annual marathon and best-known road racing events since 1986. On the same trip you may also enjoy the eight-block shopping stretch of Newbury Street in Back Bay, a block away from the library.
